Arc VIII — The Economic Decolonisation

The De-Industrialization of Bengal

The heavy, stifling monsoon humidity of the Buriganga riverfront hangs like a wet sheet over the weaving quarters of Babur Bazar. It is September 1840. The air inside the low, thatched mud workshops is thick with the scent of unrefined rice starch, damp timber oil and fermented indigo — the working atmosphere of a textile civilisation about to be legislated out of existence.

Int. Weaving Workshop — Babur Bazar, Dacca — September 15, 1840 — 14:00 Local Time

Alimullah, a master weaver of the Jamdani guild, sits inside a massive hand-carved pit-loom. Mr. Charles George, Resident Commercial Agent for the East India Company's Dacca Factory, stands under the courtyard awning with a leather-bound customs registry. The time-traveller watches from the water-logged shadow of the loom pit.

George (flat, mechanical)

It is an obsolete texture, Alimullah. The power-looms of Lancashire now produce piece-goods at a third of your cost. The contract is terminated.

Alimullah (without stopping his shuttle)

Shaheb, Lancashire-er kol chaka ghore lohar jore, ar amader ei taant chole banshher rokter taane. — Sahib, Lancashire's mill wheel turns on the force of iron, but our loom turns on the pull of generations of blood. Your machine-made cloth can cover a body, but it cannot breathe like this muslin.

George (scraping ink from his cuff)

If you sell this cloth independently to the merchants of Burrabazar, the new Internal Transit Duties apply at every river checkpoint. Eighty percent duty on your warp yarn before a single yard leaves this ghat. Manchester piece-goods enter this port at three and a half percent. You cannot out-calculate the British Parliament.

Time-Traveller (stepping from the shadow of the loom pit)

Aur George saab, is arithmetic ko barabar karne ke liye kanoon kafi nahi tha. — And Mr. George, to balance this arithmetic, the law alone was not enough. By monopolising the distribution of high-count cotton seed from Kapasia, you are intentionally starving these looms of raw material before the tariff even applies.

The scene cuts to the muddy riverfront path outside the Dacca Factory gates, where the tariff's enforcement arm finishes what the ledger began.

Ext. Dacca Factory Gates — Riverfront Path — Day

Hundreds of barefoot weavers stand soaked in monsoon rain outside the factory walls. A native gomastha holds heavy iron shears over a pile of broken shuttles. Sepoys stand in a rigid line, bayonets fixed.

Young Weaver (voice cracking)

Darogababu! Amader oporadhta ki? Amra to Company-r dadon ferot dite eshechi! — Inspector, what is our crime? We have come to return the Company's advance!

Native Gomastha (waving the shears)

Chopro! Jara shadhinbhabe deshi mohajon-der shathe chukti shoi korechhe, tara ar kono dingo Dhakar matite taant chhute parbe na! — Silence! Those who have signed contracts independently with native financiers will never touch a loom on Dacca soil again!

There is no riot — only the wet, heavy crunch of cold steel through shuttle wood, a calculated physical degradation engineered to terminate an elite lineage of craftsmanship from the human memory pool.

Back in the workshop, Alimullah lets his shuttle hang loose on a single thread. The time-traveller registers the diagnostic proof of these two chapters: the dismantling of the Dacca muslin guilds was not a natural shift of industrial progress, but a deliberate, top-down execution of an indigenous manufacturing spine, engineered to convert a continent of master creators into a captive mass of consumers.

1. The Tariff as a Manufactured Default

No power-loom in Lancashire could, on cost or quality alone, out-compete a Dacca Jamdani in 1840 — the arithmetic had to be rigged by statute. An 80% transit duty on Bengali yarn moving between native markets, set against a 3.5% duty on British piece-goods entering the same ports, was not a tariff in the ordinary sense; it was a legislated handicap sized precisely to erase a quality advantage that the market itself could never close.

2. The Parallel Strangleholds: Salt and the Sea

The same legislative architecture used against the loom was deployed, almost simultaneously, against two other independent economic loops — the coastal salt pans and the deep-sea shipyards — confirming that the Dacca campaign was not an isolated industrial accident but a template.

Int. Customs Outpost — Midnapore Coast — March 4, 1843

Mr. Edward Ravenshaw, Salt Agent for the Hidgellee Division, confronts Sanatan Malangee, hereditary chief of the local salt-boilers' guild, over brine boiled outside Company lease lines.

Sanatan (salt-crusted to the elbows)

Shaheb, shomudrer jol amader baper shompotti noy, kono Company-r dewani mohorh-o mene chole na. — Sahib, the sea's water is no man's property, nor does it answer to any Company seal. This is not theft — it is a thousand years of the right to survive.

Time-Traveller (to Ravenshaw)

Aur is stabilization ka matlab hai ki aap har ek jhopdi ko ek criminal cell bana rahe hain. — And this "stabilisation" means you are turning every hut into a criminal cell. You import Cheshire rock salt as ballast, and because it cannot compete with free solar evaporation, you use the Salt Act of 1838 to smash their clay pots instead.

Int. Shipwright's Loft — Kidderpore Docks, Calcutta — April 11, 1846

Jagannath Mistry, a master shipwright of the hereditary Oriya-Bengali naval-architect lineage, is told by Mr. Arthur Sterling, Registrar of Shipping, that the amended Navigation Acts deny deep-sea registry to any vessel built east of the Cape of Good Hope.

Jagannath (rough hands stained with tar)

Shaheb, ei segun kather toiri jahaj London-er oak kather cheye tin gun beshi din shomudrer nona jol shoite pare. — Sahib, this teak-built ship can withstand the sea's salt water three times longer than your London oak. My grandfather built the hulls that carried your own infantry to Burma. Now you call it unsafe because the wood grew in Chittagong?

Sterling (trimming a quill with a pocket-knife)

If Calcutta and Chittagong continue launching ocean-going teak vessels at half the London cost, the shipyards of the Thames and the Tyne empty out within a decade. Your ships are a phantom fleet — no legal existence on the high seas.

The historical record outside the screenplay confirms Jagannath's craft was no exaggeration. HMS Trincomalee, launched in 1817 and still afloat today in Hartlepool, England — the oldest civilian-built warship still afloat anywhere on Earth — was hand-crafted in the Bombay Dockyards by the master shipwright Jamshedji Bomanji Wadia. Indian teak hulls, sealed with a unique interlocking "clinker" joinery and resistant to the wood-boring worms that rotted English oak within twelve years, were measured at roughly 40% cheaper than London-built equivalents. In 1814, the Shipbuilders of the Port of London petitioned Parliament directly, warning that unrestricted Indian registry would ruin the Thames yards by sheer efficiency. Parliament's answer was not competition but the Registry Acts — a legal wall, not an engineering verdict, that reduced an elite maritime lineage to building river dinghies.

"Ei lohar rajatto ekdin nona dhore shesh hobe, shaheb!" — "This iron empire will one day rust and end, sahib!"

Jagannath Mistry, Master Shipwright, Kidderpore Dockyards, 1846
The Swaraj Protocol — Chapters Two & Three, Volume Seventy-Four
1. Audit the Tariff, Not the Product. When a superior local craft or product loses a market, check the regulatory fine print before assuming the market spoke — a quality advantage rarely loses without a legislated handicap behind it.
2. Rebuild the Full Production Loop. The Company did not stop at one chokepoint — it cut yarn supply, transit, registry and finance simultaneously. Rebuilding local capacity means securing raw material, distribution and certification together, not just one link in the chain.
